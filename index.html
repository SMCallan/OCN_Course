Practical Computing

A hands-on computing course where you build, test and fix real things — games, websites, micro:bit gadgets and robot ideas. There are no exams. You show what you can do, and that becomes your evidence.

Every session follows the same four steps

You always know what is coming next. Each workshop uses this routine.

1

See it

The session starts with a real challenge you can see — a broken setup, a game menu, a sensor, a robot idea.

2

Build it

You work hands-on with devices, micro:bits, websites, code blocks or design sketches.

3

Test it

Try it out. If it breaks, that is part of the plan — fixing things is a skill this course teaches.

4

Show it

Photos, screenshots, short answers or just showing your tutor. That becomes your assessment evidence.

🛡️ Unit 1 of 5 Digital Safety and Security Must do 3 credits ≈ 30 hours ▼

This unit keeps you safe for the whole course. You learn how to protect yourself and others online, how to report problems, and how to keep your accounts and files secure.

You will learn to

  • Keep your personal information private online
  • Spot problems worth reporting, and know who to tell
  • Use strong passwords, screen locks and fingerprint or face unlock
  • Check browser safety settings and site permissions
  • Back up your files and keep devices updated
  • Follow the centre's rules for safe online working

Your evidence can be

  • Short written or ticked answers in your workbook
  • Showing your tutor a privacy or security setting
  • Screenshots (with personal details hidden)
  • Spoken answers your tutor writes down

Official unit code: Y/616/8241

🔌 Unit 2 of 5 Set Up and Maintain a Digital System 3 credits ≈ 30 hours ▼

The hands-on setup unit. You build a working computer station from parts — cables, screen, keyboard, network — then keep it organised and backed up.

You will learn to

  • Connect a computer, printer and other devices using instructions
  • Use USB sticks and memory cards safely
  • Follow health and safety guidance while setting up
  • Change a setting in the user interface to suit you
  • Know how to keep a computer updated and protected
  • Organise folders, then back up and restore your files

Your evidence can be

  • Photos of your setup work
  • A screenshot of the setting you changed
  • The folder structure you created
  • Showing your tutor a backup and restore

Official unit code: A/616/8247

🔎 Unit 3 of 5 Search Engines and Online Research 2 credits ≈ 20 hours ▼

The research unit. You learn how search engines decide what to show you — then use that knowledge to find good information fast and dodge the scams and fakes.

You will learn to

  • Explain what search engines are for, and name three different ones
  • Understand why results appear in the order they do
  • Spot sponsored results and adverts in a results page
  • Research a brand-new topic using two different search engines
  • Use an advanced search trick — exact phrases, filters, searching within a site
  • Spot threats like fake websites, scam adverts and unsafe downloads

Your evidence can be

  • Your completed search tables and source notes
  • Screenshots of results (with personal details hidden)
  • Your tutor watching the safe research challenge
  • Spoken question-and-answer notes

Official unit code: T/616/8246

🌐 Unit 4 of 5 Emerging Technologies and the Internet of Things 5 credits ≈ 50 hours ▼

Explore the technology that is changing the world — AI, virtual reality, wearables, robots — then design your own “smart device” idea that connects things together.

You will learn to

  • Explain what “emerging technology” means, with examples
  • Give a benefit and a downside of a new technology
  • Explain the Internet of Things — objects that connect and share data
  • Pick a real smart product and say how it helps people
  • Design your own connected-device idea
  • Get feedback from someone and improve your idea

Your evidence can be

  • A drawing or diagram of your connected idea
  • Written or spoken answers
  • Notes from the feedback you collected
  • Your improved plan

Official unit code: L/617/9009

🎨 Unit 5 of 5 Introduction to UI and UX Design 6 credits ≈ 60 hours ▼

The creative unit. UI means the buttons, menus and screens you interact with. UX means how it feels to use them. You become a design critic — then a designer.

You will learn to

  • Explain what a user interface (UI) is
  • Use a design principle to review a real app, game menu or website
  • Suggest an improvement based on your review
  • Explain what user experience (UX) means
  • Use UX principles to suggest improvements to a second interface
  • Sketch or build your own improved screen design

Your evidence can be

  • Screenshots or drawings of the interfaces you reviewed
  • Your written or spoken review
  • A sketch, wireframe or simple webpage you made
  • Comments from someone who tested your design

Official unit code: F/617/9010

📱 Bonus unit · Optional Planning a Mobile App Extra — not needed for the certificate 5 credits ≈ 50 hours ▼

An extra unit for learners who finish early or want more. You plan your own mobile app — no coding needed. You design it, sketch the screens, get feedback and improve it.

You will learn to

  • Name different categories of apps — games, learning, health, travel
  • Review a real app and judge how easy it is to use
  • Come up with your own app idea and give it a name
  • Decide who it is for and which platform it would run on
  • List the key things your app can do
  • Collect feedback on your design and propose an improvement

Your evidence can be

  • Your app planning sheet
  • Hand-drawn or digital screen sketches
  • Feedback notes from the people you asked
  • Your improved design or written improvement plan

Official unit code: J/617/9011

📓 Unit 1 of 6 Introduction to Study Skills Must do 3 credits ≈ 27 hours ▼

Not school study skills — this is “how to run your own tech project”. You pick a project theme (a game, robot, smart device or design) and learn to manage it.

You will learn to

  • Work out how you learn best
  • See the benefits of getting involved and working with others
  • Find information and store it where you can get it back
  • Make notes in two different ways — photos, checklists, mind maps
  • Make an action plan and use it to structure your work
  • Use feedback to make one clear improvement

Your evidence can be

  • Your project notebook or evidence folder
  • Two kinds of notes you made
  • Your action plan
  • A before-and-after showing the improvement you made

Official unit code: H/508/0637

⏱️ Unit 2 of 6 Time Management Skills Must do 2 credits ≈ 18 hours ▼

Builds and projects take time. This unit gives you simple tools — timers, task boards, checklists — so your project gets finished instead of forgotten.

You will learn to

  • Say why good time management helps you
  • Spot what goes wrong when time is not managed
  • Pick a technique that works for you
  • Break a build into small tasks: set up, code, test, fix
  • Make a time plan and actually follow it
  • Review what went well and what to change next time

Your evidence can be

  • Your own time-management plan
  • A completed task board or session checklist
  • Written or spoken answers about what worked
  • Your review of what to improve

Official unit code: K/508/0638

👾 Unit 3 of 6 Introduction to Programming 3 credits ≈ 27 hours ▼

The coding unit. You plan, build and debug your own program — a micro:bit reaction game, an LED dice, a sensor alert, a score counter, or your own idea. You can use MakeCode, Scratch or Python.

You will learn to

  • Use variables and lists to store information
  • Put steps in order (sequence)
  • Make code repeat (loops)
  • Use AND, OR and NOT — like “button A AND shake”
  • Make the program choose — “if the score is over 5, you win”
  • Plan a program from a brief, then find and fix its bugs

Your evidence can be

  • Screenshots or printouts of your code
  • Showing your tutor the program working
  • Your plan: input → rule → output
  • Your debugging log — what broke and how you fixed it

Official unit code: K/616/8258

🤖 Unit 4 of 6 Introduction to Robotics 3 credits ≈ 27 hours ▼

Robots already work in hospitals, warehouses and homes. In this unit you study how — then design your own robot to solve a real problem.

You will learn to

  • Describe how robots are used in at least two industries
  • Give a benefit and a concern about robots taking on work
  • Pick a job or process a robot could do
  • Plan your robot: what it looks like, what it does, how it works
  • Check your design against what it was meant to do
  • Recommend one improvement to make it better

Your evidence can be

  • Your industry examples, written or spoken
  • Your robot design plan and sketches
  • A model, micro:bit demo or simulation if available
  • Your review and improvement idea

Official unit code: L/617/4778

📡 Unit 5 of 6 Emerging Technologies and the Internet of Things 5 credits ≈ 45 hours ▼

The big project unit. You go deeper into new technology — 3D printing, AR, VR, AI, wearables, robotics, biotechnology — then plan a complete connected-device system and improve it using real feedback.

You will learn to

  • Explain seven emerging technologies and what they do
  • Give benefits and negative impacts of at least two of them
  • Explain how Internet of Things products connect and why
  • Describe the technical connections of one real smart product
  • Plan your own connected system, step by step, with a clear benefit
  • Ask others for feedback and build one improvement into your plan

Your evidence can be

  • Your technology fact-file or comparison notes
  • A diagram mapping sensor → device → network → action
  • Your step-by-step system plan
  • Feedback notes and your updated plan

Official unit code: J/617/4777

🔧 Unit 6 of 6 Problem Solving Skills 3 credits ≈ 24 hours ▼

The debugging unit. Things break in every project — a program, a sensor, a webpage. This unit turns “it's broken” into a process you can follow calmly.

You will learn to

  • Work out what affects which fix you should choose
  • Describe more than one possible fix, with pros and cons
  • Spot the limits of each option
  • Use evidence — error messages, test results, photos
  • Choose the best fix and try it
  • Check whether it worked, and revise the plan if not

Your evidence can be

  • Your problem statement and list of options
  • The evidence you collected while testing
  • Notes on the fix you chose and why
  • Your review and revised plan

Official unit code: J/508/0694

How you are assessed — no exams

There is no exam hall and no test paper. Your tutor collects evidence of what you can do, bit by bit, during normal sessions. Any of these count:

📸

Photos & screenshots

A photo of your setup or a screenshot of your code is real evidence.

🗣️

Spoken answers

You can answer out loud. Your tutor writes it down — that counts the same as writing it yourself.

👀

Being observed

Your tutor watching you do something correctly is evidence on its own.

✍️

Short written answers

Workbook questions are short — ticking boxes, ordering steps, a sentence or two.

🎮

Demonstrations

Showing your game, program or design working is some of the strongest evidence there is.

🧑‍🤝‍🧑

Feedback from others

Notes from a classmate testing your design count towards several units.

Extra support is built in. If writing is hard, you can use spoken answers and a scribe (someone who writes for you). Every workbook has space for this — it is a normal part of the course, not a special exception.

A real qualification

💪

You can set up, fix, code, design and explain — skills that make every next step easier.
